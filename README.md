# The Cognitive Bicycle

**Honest AI Use, Intellectual Ownership, and the Framework the Academy Needs**

*Matthew John Keen — April 2026*

---

## What This Is

A working manuscript proposing **lineage reporting** as a structured institutional response to AI-assisted scholarly work. It argues that scholarly integrity depends not on rejecting new tools, but on documenting their use transparently — and that the right question is not *who produced the text* but *who directed the thinking*.

The central contribution is the **Compiler Analogy**: AI, used correctly, is a compiler for original human thought. The programmer is still the author of software they compiled. The scholar is still the author of work they directed.

The manuscript also introduces four practical **ownership tests** (Spark, Map, Voice, Defense) and a structured **Lineage Declaration** format for AI-assisted work.

## The Argument in Brief

Generative AI has changed how scholarly text is produced. It has not changed who is responsible for intellectual direction.

The **lineage** of a piece of work — the documented continuity from human intention through methodological development to final output — is what establishes scholarly trust. Disclosure of tool presence satisfies compliance. It does not constitute lineage documentation.

This manuscript proposes:

1. **The Compiler Analogy** — AI translates high-level human thought into prose, as a compiler translates high-level code into machine instructions. The human is the author in both cases, provided they supplied the intent, structure, and judgement.

2. **Four Ownership Tests** — Spark (origin of the idea), Map (structure of the argument), Voice (governing judgement), Defense (independent explainability). These are not new criteria; they are explicit statements of what good scholarship has always required.

3. **The Lineage Declaration** — a structured provenance record covering concept origin, stages of tool involvement, boundaries of assistance, and independent defence capability.

4. **The Compiler Limit** — the analogy fails where there is no direction. Using AI to generate core arguments without direction, review, or defence is not compilation. It is delegation. The framework is a defence of directed, transparent, documented AI use — not a defence of all AI use.

## On the Origin of This Work

The conceptual origins lie in two encounters with the problem of digital preservation.

The first was a visit to Microsoft, where work on long-duration archival storage technology was under discussion — technology that would eventually inform products like the Millenniata M-DISC, designed to preserve data for 1,000 years. That encounter raised the question of what such permanence might mean for fields where the survival of records is not merely a technical problem but an intellectual one.

The second was involvement with **ARKive** — a Millennium-funded global initiative supported by the UK Heritage Lottery Fund and New Opportunities Fund, creating a centralised digital archive of the world's endangered species. At its height ARKive served over half a million users monthly from more than 200 countries.

In February 2019, ARKive went offline due to sustained funding failure. The content — over 100,000 images and videos — was preserved offline. The public access was not.

ARKive is a precise illustration of the distinction this manuscript draws between tool presence and lineage preservation. The material survived. The continuity of access did not. The limiting factor was not the medium. It was the institutional will to maintain the chain.

## Declared Tool Use

A large language model (Claude, Anthropic) was used in the production of this manuscript as a research assistant, critical interlocutor, drafting instrument, and cross-disciplinary connector.

It did not originate the ideas. It did not determine the structure. It cannot defend the argument.

The AI was the compiler. The human was the author.

This declaration is made in accordance with the framework proposed in the manuscript itself.
