## What This Is

A working manuscript proposing **lineage reporting** as a structured institutional response to AI-assisted scholarly work. It argues that scholarly integrity depends not on rejecting new tools, but on documenting their use transparently — and that the right question is not *who produced the text* but *who directed the thinking*.

The central contribution is the **Compiler Analogy**: AI, used correctly, is a compiler for original human thought. The programmer is still the author of software they compiled. The scholar is still the author of work they directed.

The manuscript also introduces four practical **ownership tests** (Spark, Map, Voice, Defense) and a structured **Lineage Declaration** format for AI-assisted work.

## The Argument in Brief

Generative AI has changed how scholarly text is produced. It has not changed who is responsible for intellectual direction.

The **lineage** of a piece of work — the documented continuity from human intention through methodological development to final output — is what establishes scholarly trust. Disclosure of tool presence satisfies compliance. It does not constitute lineage documentation.

This manuscript proposes:

### 1. The Compiler Analogy

AI translates high-level human thought into prose, as a compiler translates high-level code into machine instructions. The human is the author in both cases, provided they supplied the intent, structure, and judgement.

### 2. Four Ownership Tests

Spark (origin of the idea), Map (structure of the argument), Voice (governing judgement), Defense (independent explainability). These are not new criteria; they are explicit statements of what good scholarship has always required.

### 3. The Lineage Declaration

A structured provenance record covering concept origin, stages of tool involvement, boundaries of assistance, and independent defence capability.

### 4. The Compiler Limit

The analogy fails where there is no direction. Using AI to generate core arguments without direction, review, or defence is not compilation. It is delegation. The framework is a defence of directed, transparent, documented AI use — not a defence of all AI use.
